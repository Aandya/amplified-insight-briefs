Where to Push: Meadows' Leverage Points

Twelve places to intervene in a system, ranked from weakest to strongest — and a real distressed company that shows why it matters which one you choose.
Donella Meadows' hierarchy · applied to the CG Power crisis of 2019

Every system — a company, an economy, a household — can be changed at many points. Donella Meadows' great insight was that these points are not equal, and that we are drawn, almost magnetically, to the weakest of them. We argue about budgets and prices and targets while the real levers — who controls the system, what it is for, the assumptions it rests on — sit untouched above our heads.

What follows is the full ladder, from the weakest rung to the strongest, each with a plain explanation and two examples — one from everyday life, one from business. Then a real case: a company where management insisted the answer lay at the bottom of the ladder, while the cure sat near the top.

Part I · The Twelve Leverage Points

Ranked strongest (top) to weakest (bottom). Lower number = higher leverage. Select any rung to expand.
+ Select any rung to read its explanation and examples
↑ Strongest leverage · hardest to pull
LP 1HighThe power to transcend paradigms+
Holding no single worldview as absolute — the freedom to choose the frame itself.

The highest and rarest leverage: to stay unattached to any one model of how the world works, and so be able to step outside it. It cannot be forced, only practised.

EverydayThe stance of the reformer or the meditator — seeing that every belief, including one's own, is a lens and not the territory.
BusinessA founder who can abandon the very model that made them successful when the world changes, rather than defend it to the end.
LP 2HighThe paradigm — the system's shared mindset+
The unspoken assumptions from which goals, rules and structure all flow.

Paradigms are the deepest set of beliefs about how things are. Shift the paradigm and the goals, rules and parameters reorganise themselves around it.

Everyday"Growth is always good." "Nature is property." Whole societies are built on assumptions no one votes on.
Business"A promoter's companies are one shared purse." Change that belief to "a company is a fiduciary trust for all its shareholders," and behaviour transforms.
LP 3HighGoals — the purpose of the system+
What the system is actually for. Change the goal and everything realigns.

A system's true goal is revealed by its behaviour, not its mission statement. Redirect the goal and the same people, assets and rules produce a different world.

EverydayA health system aimed at "treating illness" behaves utterly differently from one aimed at "producing health."
BusinessA company whose real goal is "extract cash for the owner" versus one whose goal is "build an independent, durable business." Same factory, opposite outcomes.
LP 4HighSelf-organisation — power to change the structure+
The system's ability to add, change or evolve its own structure, rules and controllers.

The capacity for a system to reshape itself — through new leadership, new ownership, innovation, evolution. The most powerful concrete intervention available to us.

EverydayEvolution and innovation — a living system rewriting its own design over time.
BusinessA change of control: a new and credible owner empowered to restructure the board, the capital, and the rules from the top down.
LP 5MidRules — incentives, constraints, punishments+
The rules of the game: who may do what, who is rewarded, who is restrained.

Rules define the space of allowed behaviour. Change them — laws, contracts, property rights, governance codes — and behaviour across the whole system shifts.

EverydayA constitution; the rules of a sport. Change the offside rule and the whole game reorganises.
BusinessRelated-party-transaction rules with real teeth; loan covenants; an audit committee that can actually say no.
LP 6MidInformation flows — who can see what+
Adding or restoring feedback of information to those who act on it.

Much dysfunction comes from missing feedback. Give the right actors the right information and behaviour self-corrects — often cheaply.

EverydayPutting the electricity meter where people can see it cuts usage. Sunlight is a disinfectant.
BusinessA forensic audit; public disclosure; a real-time dashboard placed in front of the people who can act.
LP 7MidReinforcing feedback loops — the gain+
Self-amplifying loops — virtuous or vicious circles. Reduce their gain.

Reinforcing loops drive runaway growth or collapse. Slowing the loop's gain is higher leverage than strengthening the balancing loop that fights it.

EverydayCompound interest; erosion that accelerates; "the rich get richer."
BusinessA debt spiral, or "success to the successful" — one division starved so a favoured one is fed, widening the gap each cycle.
LP 8MidBalancing feedback loops+
The self-correcting loops that hold a system near a target.

Balancing loops keep systems stable. Their strength must match the disturbances they correct — too weak and the system drifts; restore them and it self-regulates.

EverydayA thermostat; the body sweating to cool itself; a market price correcting supply and demand.
BusinessA regulator, an internal control, or a covenant — but only when it actually bites.
LP 9LowDelays — the lengths of lags+
The time between an action and its effect, relative to the rate of change.

Delays cause overshoot and oscillation. They are often impossible to change — and where you can't, you must respect them rather than overcorrect.

EverydayA shower with a slow hot-water lag — you keep overcorrecting and scald, then freeze.
BusinessA 90-day standstill agreement; long reporting lags that let a problem grow unseen.
LP 10LowStock-and-flow structure+
The physical plumbing — how the system is built and connected.

The physical arrangement of a system. Powerful when it is the real bottleneck, but usually slow and expensive to change — sometimes the only honest fix.

EverydayA city's road network; the layout of water pipes. You can't re-plumb overnight.
BusinessRe-laying a production line; redesigning a supply chain's physical nodes.
LP 11LowBuffers — the size of stabilising stocks+
The size of cushions that stabilise a system relative to its flows.

Bigger buffers make a system more stable — but also more sluggish and costly. Adjusting them rarely changes the system's fundamental behaviour.

EverydayA larger water reservoir smooths droughts but is expensive and slow to fill.
BusinessHolding more safety stock or a bigger cash reserve — comfort, not transformation.
LP 12LowNumbers — constants and parameters+
The dials and figures: prices, budgets, tax rates, limits, targets.

The weakest lever, and the one we reach for first. Changing a number almost never changes the system's behaviour — the structure simply regenerates the old outcome.

EverydayNudging the thermostat; a small change to the minimum wage or a tax rate.
BusinessTrimming a budget line by five percent; adjusting a credit limit. It feels productive and changes little.
↓ Weakest leverage · easiest to pull · where we crowd

Part II · Why We Crowd the Bottom

Meadows' uncomfortable finding is that the levers are inversely related to our instincts. The numbers at the bottom are visible, quantifiable, and politically safe — so that is where committees gather and consultants are pointed. The levers at the top are abstract, contested, and threatening to whoever holds power — so they are left alone. The result is enormous effort spent where systems barely move.

"The trouble is, we know where to push, and we push there with all our might — at exactly the wrong points."

Part III · The Case — CG Power, 2019

A real listed manufacturer where every fix management offered sat at the bottom of the ladder — and the cure sat near the top.

The situation. CG Power & Industrial Solutions made motors and transformers — a viable business with a real order book. Yet its shares had collapsed, its banks had frozen its credit lines, and its own investigation had surfaced large unauthorised, related-party transactions. Management's account was confident: a liquidity problem, a sector slowdown, panicked lenders. Fix the cash, restore the limits, ride out the noise.

The real structure. Beneath the scandal sat a captured system. The promoter ran the company as a captive financing vehicle: its own assets and deposits had been pledged as security for the promoter group's borrowings, and its cash advanced to related entities — with a board that could not, and would not, say no. A reinforcing loop was running:

Why the offered fixes were doomed. Pour fresh liquidity into that loop — restore the bank limits, as management begged — and you simply feed it. The unchanged structure drains it again next quarter. You are bailing water from a boat with the tap still running. Look at where each proposed fix actually sat:

What actually moved it. In 2020, Tube Investments of India — part of the Murugappa Group — took control through a fresh injection of equity. A credible owner replaced the old one. That single act operated near the top of the ladder: it changed who held the power to reshape the system (level 4), which flipped the company's real goal from financing reservoir to independent business (level 3), and quietly challenged the paradigm that a group's companies are one purse (level 2). The capital was the fuel; the change of control was the leverage. Capital alone, poured into the old structure, would have leaked away like everything before it. CG Power went on to become one of the market's notable recoveries.

The portable test — so you never aim too low

  1. Will the existing structure simply absorb or reverse this? If yes, you are too low on the ladder. Climb.
  2. Am I changing a number, or changing who gets to set the numbers? Changing the number is the weakest lever there is. Changing who sets it is among the strongest.
Habit for any distress diagnosis: walk the ladder from the top down — paradigm, goal, who controls, rules, information — before you let anyone drag you to buffers and parameters. Every other advisor starts at the bottom. Start at the top. And remember: the altitude of the lever someone urges on you is itself a diagnostic — when a person in trouble insists the answer is tinkering with the numbers, ask what structure that tinkering would conveniently leave untouched.
